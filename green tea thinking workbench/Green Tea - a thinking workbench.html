Mind the seam: your field runs on more than one standard

The hard question is rarely “is this output good?” It's “which standard governs here?” A model blends frameworks into one confident voice and is least trustworthy exactly where they meet.

“Green tea is safe, it's been used for centuries, and clinical pharmacokinetic studies show low interaction risk for most drugs.”

Two different standards are spliced together. “Used for centuries” is a traditional-use claim; “PK studies show low interaction risk” is a pharmacological-evidence claim. Neither validates the other, long use doesn't establish PK safety, and a PK study doesn't speak to a constitutional or whole-system tradition. A model will hand you one smooth verdict that hides the join. Your job is to split them and judge each on its own terms.

Record your model What you get is your skill times the model's capability, you can't read one without fixing the other. Note which model and tier you used (the way a stats course pins R or SPSS). The same prompt on a weaker tier is a different experiment.

Module 02 · Establishing context

Two Kinds of Unsafe

Dr. Gurley spends the lecture on one kind of danger; your essay's third question asks about a different kind. If you don't separate them, you'll answer the liver question with drug-interaction evidence. This module pulls the two axes apart.

The fork A botanical can be “unsafe” in two unrelated ways. It can change what a co-administered drug does. That is an interaction, where the drug is the victim. Or it can damage the host directly, with no drug involved at all. That is intrinsic toxicity. The two need different evidence and different arguments.

Sort the cases

Each card is a real example from the lecture (plus two from the “other mechanisms” aside). Pick the mechanism. You'll get the reasoning either way.

Case 1. A transplant patient on cyclosporine starts St. John's wort. Her drug levels crash and she rejects the graft.

Case 2. Goldenseal is co-taken with a CYP3A4 substrate. The drug clears more slowly and its blood levels climb.

Case 3. Black pepper extract (piperine) is added to nevirapine. The drug's exposure (AUC) jumps sharply.

Case 4. Green tea is taken with the beta-blocker nadolol. Nadolol is poorly absorbed and blood-pressure control is lost. (This is the lecture's actual green-tea example.)

Case 5. Ginkgo is taken alongside warfarin. Bleeding risk rises, but neither drug's blood level has changed.

Case 6. Someone takes a concentrated green-tea catechin weight-loss capsule on an empty stomach and develops liver injury. No other medication is involved.

Why this matters for your week Cases 1–5 are all interactions, where the drug is the victim. Case 6 is intrinsic toxicity. The lecture's green-tea segment is Case 4 (an interaction with nadolol). But your discussion and essay Question 3, “evidence for green tea being hepatotoxic, and which preparation is riskier,” is Case 6. Reasoning from the nadolol example to the liver question is a category error. And notice Cases 3 vs 4: both are “inhibition” with opposite outcomes, because one hits a metabolizing enzyme and the other an uptake pump.

The therapeutic window, for the interaction cases

For interactions that change blood levels (Cases 1–3), the stakes are visible on one picture: the band a drug has to stay inside to work without harming.

  • Normal dosing: the peak sits inside the band, effective and safe.

Text equivalent: induction (more enzyme/transporter made) clears the drug faster, so the curve sinks below the minimum-effective line, the drug stops working. Inhibition (less enzyme activity) lets the drug accumulate, so the curve rises above the maximum-safe line, the drug turns toxic.

Module 03 · Metacognition

Interrogation Gym

The discussion names a method, interrogation (Harvard's Six Reading Habits), and then links out to it. Here you practice the moves on a throwaway text, so that when you turn the method on the HerbalGram article it's already in your hands.

What interrogation is Reading a text by putting questions to it instead of absorbing it: What is being claimed? What actually supports the claim? What is assumed but never stated? What's missing? Compared to what, and who says so?

Part A, Tag the moves

Below is a fabricated passage about a made-up plant, “Silverleaf.” (Fabricated on purpose, so you can't and won't cite it.) Open each sentence and label it: is it a claim, the evidence, or a hidden assumption? There isn't always one right tag, the win is the reason you give.

Notice the shape: one thin data point (sentence 2), an over-reaching conclusion built on it (3), a contradicting anecdote (4), and two sentences held up entirely by unstated premises (1, 5). A confident paragraph can be almost empty of usable evidence.

Part B, Turn the gaps into questions

Interrogation doesn't stop at “this is weak.” It converts each gap into a question you could actually go answer. Tick every category the Silverleaf passage leaves unanswered, then build the question set.

    Now do it for real Run the same two passes on the HerbalGram article: tag what's claim vs evidence vs assumption, then generate at least three missing-data questions. That's literally Part 2 of your discussion, but the questions have to be yours, about green tea, not Silverleaf.

    Module 04 · Metacognition · seeds Week 10

    Confidence Dial

    A verdict is only as good as the confidence attached to it. This module builds one habit: state a claim, set your confidence, and refuse to hold it until you can name the strongest case against it and what would change your mind.

    The rule Confidence should track the balance of evidence, not how good the claim sounds. If you can't say what would move you off a position, you don't hold a calibrated belief, you hold a commitment.

    0% = I'd bet against it · 50% = coin flip · 100% = I'd stake the grade on it

    Diagnosis

    Calibrated

    Your confidence roughly matches the opposition you can see.

    Why now Week 10 asks you to commit to a hypothesis on kratom and defend it. A defensible verdict is exactly this: a confidence that moves with evidence, plus a stated falsifier. Build the reflex on low stakes here.

    Module 05 · Establishing context

    Dose ≠ Dose

    Two of the discussion questions hinge on quantities the lecture never makes explicit: do animal doses transfer to humans, and which preparation is riskier? Both are reasoning problems, not lookups. This module builds the reasoning, with deliberately illustrative numbers.

    Read this first Every number here is a round, illustrative figure for teaching the logic. None of it is citable, and none of it answers your essay. The scaling formula is standard; the per-cup and per-capsule amounts are ballpark. Take the shape of the reasoning, not the digits.

    A. Why an animal dose isn't a human dose

    You can't read a milligram-per-kilogram dose straight across species. A smaller animal runs a faster metabolism per kilogram, so the same mg/kg hits it less hard. The standard fix scales by body-surface area (the FDA “human-equivalent dose” method). Watch what a big animal dose becomes in a person.

    Human-equivalent dose: 162 mg/kg

    11,300 mg

    for a 70 kg adult, about 160 cups of brewed tea (~70 mg catechins each) or 28 concentrated-extract capsules (~400 mg each).

    A 1000 mg/kg rat dose is frightening on paper, but the human-equivalent is unreachable by drinking tea. The animal number warns; it doesn't translate one-to-one.

    B. Why the same compound, two forms, is two risks

    Total dose isn't the whole story. A concentrated extract delivers a sharp bolus; brewed tea is dilute and sipped. An empty stomach raises catechin absorption. Move the controls and watch peak exposure relative to one cup of tea taken with food.

That distinction, not a single yes/no, is what your Question 3 answer has to be built on. Go find the real figures yourself.

    Module 07 · Metacognition

    Real Risk vs Reported Risk

    Your essay's biggest single criterion (40 points) asks for one narrative that holds two things at once: the real toxicity risk and the bias that was reported about it. Most drafts blur them. This module trains the split before you write.

    Three buckets, not two A statement about the danger itself is a claim about real risk. A statement about how sources talk about the danger is about reported risk. And a competing cause that muddies who's to blame is a confound, the thing that lets you separate the first two. Sort each card.
